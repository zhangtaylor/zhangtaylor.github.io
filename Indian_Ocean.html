The Ocean

The Indian Ocean

The Indian Ocean is the 3rd largest ocean, and was first explored around
the 1500s to find a route around Africa. It was used as an important trade route
to trade silk. It has the saltiest and unsaltiest waters in the world and is
responsible for around 40% of oil. It has limited sea life due to the low oxygen
content, but still has interesting animals either way.


A Brief History

In the 1500s, the Indian Ocean was used to find a route around African and
was already an important silk trade route. It was mostly dominated in the UK in
the early 19th century, but was later conquered by Australia and Indian. Today,
many ports are built in the Indian Ocean and different countries conquer different
parts of the ocean.


Animals

Unlike other oceans, the Indian Ocean cannot support much marine life.
The temperature makes it difficult for phytoplankton to grow, which is an
important food source for ecosystems. The ocean also has low oxygen content,
which contributes to the low sea life. Nevertheless, there are still many animals
that live in the ocean, like the African Penguin, the only penguing species in African.
There are also many species of turtles and tortoise, the dugong (a mammal related to
the manatee), and the now extinct dodo bird. There are many different types of sharks and
whales, crabs, insects, and fish. Pelicans, sea lions, urchins, snails, sponges, and sea slugs.
There are also puffer fish, the second most poisonous creature in the world!


Fun Facts

  • The water evaporates at a high rate due to the ocean's temperature
  • The Indian Ocean is very polluted due to the oil and ship pollution
  • Monsoons are a common occurence
  • It is estimated the Indian Ocean becomes 20cm wider per year
